Exploring my Favorite Stories

By Ben Soroka

The Way of Kings The Boys About Me Home

Summary

The Way of Kings is the first book in Brandon Sanderson's The Stormlight Archive. It follows multiple characters that navigate a world that is greatly unlike our own: Roshar, which is shaped by war, ancient heroes, and massive storms. Kaladin is the first of the main protagonists we meet, and is a soldier turned slave that is struggling to find his purpose. Shallan shows us the scholarly side of the world and helps explain some of the mechanics behind the magic. Lastly, Dalinar is a general that receives visions of an ancient order of knights and how they are destined to return. The book bounces between these three perspectives, in addition to several others, which all combine to tell what could be argued as the greatest tale told to date.

Purpose of the Story

Sanderson has just released the fifth book of The Stormlight Archive, Wind and Truth. In his Q&A, a fan said "on your podcast, you were talking about authors who you felt wrote something revolutionary, and literary, and beautiful that changed peoples' lives. You mentioned that you felt you had not quite reached that point. I'm wondering what do you feel you need to do to reach that point and do you have any plans to get there?"
Sanderson responded, "The reason I can't say that I got there is that I think that ... 50 years after they [renowned authors] passed away, looking back ... things that have sold as well ... have done so until we're 50 years past it. I certainly can say, like, Books change lives. I can point at, for instance, Dragonsbane changing my life ... did it change the face of fantasy? No it did not. ... Most of us don't write something that changes the nature of a genre; that's okay. We're here to change peoples lives, and if you only write a few books and it changes the lives, in some way, of your friends and family, then that's a powerful, important book. You don't have to change the face of publishing." <
Sanderson said in the same interview, "I don't know what I would do with myself if I wasn't writing" . Therefore, we can likely ascertain that Sanderson wrote this and all his other books for two purposes: The first being because he loves writing, and the second being that he wishes to change not the lives of the many, but just the lives of the few. He finds fulfillment in knowing that even if his books don't become worldwide phenomena like Harry Potter (Although at this point they have), he is glad to know they helped some people get through tough times and that his work left a positive impact.

Influences/Connections

In the same interview, Sanderson was asked "Where do you get the inspiration to make all your books?". His reply was "Every one of them is different. I would say I was mostly inspired by the books I read when I was younger. ... At the same time, every time I write a book, I have a different method of coming about why I decided to write it. Some ... start because I watch a movie and ask "what if?" ... some of them come about because I realize 'hey, a really powerful magical hurricane hitting a planet would do really interesting things to the ecology.' "
In terms of connections to stories we have learned in this class, I would say that our first module on folklore, myths, and fairy tales most certainly have had a massive impact on The Way of Kings and the fantasy genre in general. HOW TROUBLES CAME INTO THE WORLD brings up mythology and things such as gods, which are showcased in pretty much any piece of fantasy media ever created. The Fisherman and the Genie showcases the allure of the mystical and how man can interact with the arcane. Lastly, The Legends of King Arthur and his Knights clearly had a large inspiration on this specific books, as the Knights Radiant are seemingly very inspired by the tale of King Arthur. Dalinar symbolizes Arthur, the chosen man of honor who embodies the ideals of justice and unity, and the rest of the Knights Radiant represent the Round table. I would be shocked if Sanderson said he was not influenced by this story.

Cultural/Societal Relevance

This book tackles and addresses many contemporary topics, such as mental health, class inequality, and gender roles.

Mental Health

Kaladin struggles with depression and at multiple points attempts to end his own life. Eventually (In a later book), Dalinar recognizes Kaladin's struggles and forces him to take a temporary leave from the army to deal with his mental health. Shallan also has a mental disorder, which develops more and more as we progress through the stories. It appears to be Dissociative Identity Disorder (Multiple Personality Disorder), but the books show how this disorder is not a disability and how it helps her cope with her traumatic past and get things done.

Class Inequality

In Alethkar (The country where most of Kaladin and Dalinar's stories take place), people with light eyes are the privileged upper-class, and those with dark eyes are subjugated. Kaladin shows the world by becoming one of the first Knight Radiants that anyone can do anything, but is not promoted very high into the army at first (Even though he singlehandedly won a war) because Dalinar fears backlash from the other lighteyes.

Gender Roles

Men in Alethkar are largely illiterate, and therefore most women are expected to be scribes that handle written communication. Women are also expected to keep their "safehand" (left hand) covered, which is similar to some cultures/religions in our world which expect women to dress modestly. Lastly, women are largely expected to not be in power and to avoid dominating "masculine spaces" such as politics and physical danger. The book challenges many of these issues. Dalinar eventually decides to learn to read and write, to the distaste of many lighteyes. Also, Shallan becomes a Knight Radiant and takes over a branch of the organization, not only taking for herself a position of power but engaging in combat and sabotage.

Narrative Strategies

Sanderson uses point of view in a beautiful way to build his world. He rotates through viewpoints, which allows readers to see the world through multiple perspectives. He even has several interludes which include unnamed characters in faraway lands and further flesh out the world of Roshar. Additionally, each chapter starts with an important quote from a book or epitaphs that even further tell us about the world. There is a lot of symbolism and some nonlinearity as we go back in time to see Kaladin, Shallan, and Dalinar, and even some other characters' pasts.

Why I picked this story

I believe The Way of Kings is the cornerstone of modern fantasy storytelling. Sanderson's blend of worldbuilding, philosophy, and struggle combine together to create something truly incredible and unique. When I first read this book, I was truly stunned by how spectacular it is. This book, interestingly enough, opened my eyes to many real-world issues that I was not aware of prior to reading it. It's slow and deliberate, but not painfully so, and the end of the book makes it all come together in such a satisfying way! This is one of my favorite books of all time, and I highly recommend everyone to give it a read.
